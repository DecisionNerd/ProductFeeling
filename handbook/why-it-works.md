# Why it Works

Feeling is not a coat of paint on top of “real” product work. It is how people encode value, decide what to try next, and choose whether to return. This handbook treats emotion as a design requirement—measured through behaviour and memory—while refusing manipulative dark patterns that manufacture urgency, addiction, or shame.

Working definitions of the science below live in [Concepts](concepts/index.md). This page is the lineage and the why—not a second textbook. Know *who* you are designing for first: [Customer Discovery](discovery/index.md).

## The science of product feeling

Emotion and cognition are coupled. Affect changes how people think under load: positive affect tends to broaden attention and raise tolerance for minor [friction](concepts/04-friction.md); negative affect narrows focus and makes the same interface feel harder (Norman and related work on [emotional design](concepts/06-emotional-design.md)). That is why a calm first success can unlock exploration, while a confusing empty state or opaque wait can freeze a session even when the underlying capability is fine.

People also do not remember experiences as averages. The **[peak–end rule](concepts/07-peak-end-rule.md)** (Kahneman, Fredrickson, and colleagues) shows that remembered quality is disproportionately shaped by the most intense moments and by how an episode ends. Product moments—first value, a broken promise, a fair paywall, a recovery after error—become the story users tell themselves later. Designing for feeling therefore means designing peaks and endings deliberately, not only funnels.

Appearance and craft change judgement too. The **[aesthetic–usability effect](concepts/08-aesthetic-usability-effect.md)** (Kurosu & Kashimura, 1995; later Tractinsky and others) finds that people rate more attractive interfaces as more usable, and often forgive small flaws when the product feels considered. Beauty does not replace clarity—but ugliness amplifies every stumble. [Premium Positioning](strategies/12-premium-positioning.md) and [Experience Refinement](strategies/08-experience-refinement.md) sit on that mechanism: earned craft signals, not decoration that hides broken jobs.

Habit and return behaviour rest on similar ground—see [Habit Formation](concepts/10-habit-formation.md). Context-cued routines form when a stable cue, a doable action, and a meaningful outcome repeat together. Variable novelty can decorate a reliable loop; it should not *be* the loop. Commitment and consistency research explains why voluntary, low-stakes declarations increase follow-through—and why forced pledges poison trust.

Across these findings runs one design implication: **[agency](concepts/12-user-agency.md) preserves feeling**. People attach to products that make them feel competent, clear, and in control—not cornered. Name the [feeling north star](concepts/01-feeling-north-star.md) first; then choose [Tools, Techniques, and Practices (TTPs)](concepts/02-tools-techniques-practices.md) that earn it: shorten [time to value](ttps/time-to-value.md), make waits and risks honest ([calibrated trust](concepts/11-calibrated-trust.md)), invite intent instead of extracting it, and measure whether “activated” or “retained” still means the user is better off.


## From usability to feeling

Emotionally aware product work did not start with consumer apps. It is a lineage of human–computer interaction (HCI), psychology, and design practice that gradually made *feeling* an explicit object of design.

**Usability first (1980s–1990s).** Donald Norman’s *The Design of Everyday Things* and contemporary usability research (Nielsen and others) established that products fail when people cannot form a clear [mental model](concepts/05-mental-models.md). The emotional outcome of that era was mostly negative-space: reduce confusion, error, and helplessness. Competence and calm were the feelings—even if the vocabulary was “usability,” not “emotion.”

**Apparent beauty and perceived ease (mid-1990s).** Kurosu and Kashimura’s automated teller machine (ATM) studies showed that aesthetic appeal strongly shaped *perceived* usability—the finding later named the [aesthetic–usability effect](concepts/08-aesthetic-usability-effect.md). Craft stopped being “nice to have” and became part of the experience model.

**Affective computing (late 1990s).** Rosalind Picard’s *Affective Computing* argued that systems should recognise and respond to human affect. Much of that research was sensing and machine response; the product lesson that travelled further was simpler: interfaces that ignore frustration, confusion, or delight are incomplete models of the human on the other side of the glass.

**Emotional design as a named craft (2000s).** Norman’s *Emotional Design* (2004) gave practitioners a shared frame—see [Emotional Design](concepts/06-emotional-design.md): **visceral**, **behavioural**, and **reflective**. Microinteractions (Saffer and contemporaries) made the behavioural level concrete: tiny acknowledgements that turn cold software into something that feels alive.

**Behaviour, habit, and growth practice (2010s).** Behavioural science entered mainstream product work through habit models, commitment techniques, and growth loops—useful when it explained return ([Habit Formation](concepts/10-habit-formation.md)); harmful when it licensed addiction patterns. [Jobs-to-be-Done](concepts/09-jobs-to-be-done.md) reframed copy and flows around the progress people hire a product to make. Product-led growth operationalised time to value and activation as the feeling that the product worked.

**Where ProductFeeling sits.** This handbook inherits that prior art and packages it for builders and agents: [Customer Discovery](discovery/index.md) as prerequisite, [Concepts](concepts/index.md) as shared vocabulary, twelve [strategies](strategies/index.md), and thirty-seven [TTPs](ttps/index.md). It sits upstream of **Impeccable** (craft and execution) and beside **DocSlime** (docs)—naming the [feeling north star](concepts/01-feeling-north-star.md) first, then the patterns that earn it, without prescribing emotional coercion.

## An Art or Science?

Both—and the two are not in tension. Designing how a product feels is an **art grounded in precise measurement and the rigorous use of [TTPs](concepts/02-tools-techniques-practices.md)**. The taste is yours; the technique is disciplined.

Consider the French chef. No one reasonable would argue that a chef practising *cuisine* in a fine-dining room is not making art. Yet look at how they are trained. In the canonical texts used throughout France—Escoffier's *Le Guide Culinaire* and the culinary-school curriculum built on it—even something as humble as cutting a vegetable is codified: the *julienne*, *brunoise*, *bâtonnet*, and *macédoine* are named cuts with specified dimensions, down to the millimetre. A student is drilled on knife work on carrots and cucumbers long before they plate anything. The rigour is not the enemy of the art—it is the floor that makes the art repeatable, teachable, and reliably excellent.

Product feeling works the same way. The [feeling north star](concepts/01-feeling-north-star.md) is the dish you intend; the [TTPs](ttps/index.md) are the named cuts; behaviour and memory are how you *taste as you go*—[peak–end](concepts/07-peak-end-rule.md) moments, [friction](concepts/04-friction.md) budgets, [trust](concepts/11-calibrated-trust.md) movements, return curves. You measure not to reduce the work to a formula, but to know whether the feeling you intended is the feeling people actually get.

Two failure modes bracket the craft, and both ruin the plate:

- **Consumed by process.** Executing techniques flawlessly while forgetting the dish. A perfectly scored, perfectly instrumented flow that evokes nothing is not a success—it is *mise en place* mistaken for dinner.
- **Lost in vibes.** Trusting taste alone, unmeasured, so bias and wishful thinking pass for judgement. "It feels great" with no evidence is how teams ship things only they love.

The discipline of this handbook is the narrow path between them: name the feeling, choose the techniques that earn it, and measure honestly—so your art is grounded, not guessed. That is the whole point of the north star: **grounded clarity, not vibes.**

- [Emotion & Design: Attractive things work better (Don Norman)](https://jnd.org/emotion-design-attractive-things-work-better/) — How positive and negative affect change problem-solving and tolerance for friction.
- [Emotional Design: People and Things (Don Norman)](https://jnd.org/emotional-design-people-and-things/) — Visceral, behavioural, and reflective levels as a practical product frame.
- [The Aesthetic-Usability Effect (Nielsen Norman Group)](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/aesthetic-usability-effect/) — Why craft raises perceived usability—and can mask real friction in research.
- [The Peak–End Rule (NN/g)](https://www.nngroup.com/articles/peak-end-rule/) — Why intense moments and endings dominate remembered experience.
- [Affective Computing group (MIT Media Lab)](https://www.media.mit.edu/groups/affective-computing/overview/) — Picard’s lineage treating affect as a first-class signal between human and system.
- [Psychology of Habit (Wood & Rünger, 2016)](https://doi.org/10.1146/annurev-psych-122414-033417) — How context-cued habits form—and why return design is not the same as compulsion.
