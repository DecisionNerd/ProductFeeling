# Spark Curiosity


Offer a truthful glimpse of a useful outcome, then let users choose whether to explore it.

## What it is

A partial preview of information, capability, or progress that creates a genuine question. The user can understand what is being hinted at, why it matters, and how to resolve it.

## Why it works

People often want to close meaningful knowledge gaps. Curiosity works when the missing information serves a user goal and the reveal delivers real value—not when the product withholds something merely to create pressure.

## When to use it

- To preview a result users have meaningfully contributed to creating
- To introduce optional depth after a useful first outcome
- To invite exploration in discovery, learning, or creative products

## Do

- Reveal enough context for users to judge whether exploring is worthwhile
- State the next step and what it will unlock
- Resolve the question promptly once users choose to continue

## Don't

- Hide essential information, prices, messages, or safety-relevant details
- Use false scarcity, ambiguous notifications, or endless teasing
- Make curiosity the only route to understand the product’s value

## Founder Tip

A good tease makes the next action clearer. If it only makes users anxious, it is not curiosity—it is friction.

## Make It Yours

1. What real question would a user naturally want answered here?
2. What context lets them decide whether the answer is worth pursuing?
3. What action resolves the preview, and is that action proportionate?
4. Can users decline or defer without losing essential value?
5. Does the reveal deliver a useful outcome rather than another teaser?
