# Graceful Exit


Make leaving—cancelling, pausing, exporting, deleting—as respectful as joining, so the last impression earns the return visit.

## What it is

The designed off-ramp: cancellation that takes about as much effort as signup, a genuine pause option, data export people can actually use, and account deletion that does what it says. It treats departure as a lifecycle stage to design, not a leak to plug.

## Why it works

Endings dominate memory. A user who leaves easily, with their data and dignity, tells a different story than one who fought through a cancellation maze—and is far more likely to return or recommend. Hard-to-cancel patterns (“roach motel”) are among the most litigated dark patterns; regulators on both sides of the Atlantic now treat obstruction as unfair practice, not clever retention.

## When to use it

- Subscription cancellation, downgrade, and pause flows
- Account deletion and data-export requests
- Offboarding from a team, workspace, or plan
- Win-back messaging after a respectful departure

## Do

- Put cancel, pause, and delete where users expect them—inside settings, self-serve, few steps
- Offer a genuine pause or downgrade once, clearly, with skip equal in weight
- Confirm what happens to data, billing, and access in plain language before the final step
- Let people take their work with them in a usable format
- End on goodwill: confirm completion immediately and leave the door visibly open

## Don't

- Require a phone call, chat queue, or sales conversation to cancel what was one click to buy
- Chain guilt screens, hidden links, or “are you sure?” loops (cancellation sludge)
- Use confirmshaming copy (“I don’t like saving money”)
- Keep billing while claiming cancellation is processing
- Punish departure by holding data hostage

## Founder Tip

Count the clicks to subscribe and the clicks to cancel. If the second number is bigger, you are renting resentment, not retaining customers. Intelligence handlers end an asset relationship **firm, fair, friendly, final**—the same four words make a complete exit-flow review: the cancellation happens when asked (firm), data and billing are settled cleanly (fair), the last screen is goodwill (friendly), and the user leaves knowing exactly where things stand (final).

## Make It Yours

1. Walk your own cancellation path today—how many steps, and how does each one feel?
2. What would a genuinely useful pause or downgrade look like for your product’s natural usage rhythm?
3. What do departing users need to take with them, and is the export actually usable?
4. What does the final screen say—and would you be proud to see it quoted in a review?
5. How many cancelled users return within a year, and does that differ by how the exit felt?
